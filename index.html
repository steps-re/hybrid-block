The Hybrid Block

A week built on the Olympic lifts for raw power, your bike for the engine, and a touch of physique on top — none of them cannibalizing the others. The organizing rule: the barbell speed comes first, so everything else is arranged to keep your legs and nervous system fresh for it.

01

Why it's built this way

The snatch and clean & jerk are speed-and-skill lifts — they need a fresh nervous system and snappy legs, and they're the first thing a fatigued week steals from you. So the whole layout is arranged to protect the barbell, then fit the engine and the aesthetics into the space around it.

Protect the speed

Olympic lifting lives on days 1 & 3, right off rest, when your legs and CNS are freshest. Explosive power is trained fresh or not at all — you can't grind a fast lift into existence.

One hard ride, well spaced

Your only intense bike session sits far from both lifting days. Hard intervals on tired legs, or heavy squats on ride-fatigued legs, is where the two goals start eating each other.

Keep most miles easy

The bike is your engine and your recovery, so the bulk of it stays true Zone 2. High-volume junk intensity is the fastest way to blunt both your squat and your snatch.

Put the vanity up top

Physique work is loaded onto the upper body — arms, delts, chest, back. The lifts already hammer your legs, traps and posterior chain, so this fills gaps instead of adding leg fatigue.

02

The training week

Two Olympic days built on squats, one upper-body strength/physique day, three rides (two easy, one hard), one true rest day. Move days to fit your calendar — just keep the two lifting days off the back of your hard ride, and both off the back of rest.

03

The barbell work, and how to run it

The clean & jerk is your heavy power lift; the snatch rides along as skill work until it earns the right to be loaded. Pulls build a pull stronger than the lift, and the squats are the engine room underneath both. Percentages are of your best in that lift.

LiftRoleHow to run it
Clean & JerkYour heavy power lift + overhead strengthWhere you push the weight. Heavy single, then 4×(1+1) @ ~80%. Reset the jerk dip — it's a separate skill.
Snatch (skill)Trained as a skill you're building, not a max you testLight, fast, frequent. Power/hang snatch 5×2 at a weight you never miss. Chase positions and speed, not the number.
Snatch / Clean PullsBuilds a pull heavier than your lifts3×3 @ 90–100% of the matching lift. Finish tall, stay over the bar.
Back SquatThe strength base under everything4×4 @ ~83%. This is what raises the ceiling on your Oly numbers.
Front SquatClean-specific legs + upright torso4×3. Elbows high; carries straight into the clean recovery.
Push Press / JerkOverhead power — and free delt work3×5. Drive with the legs; doubles as aesthetic shoulder volume.

Getting better at the snatch: nearly everyone who's "bad at snatches" only ever tries them heavy and occasionally — which is exactly how you don't learn a skill lift. Flip it: light, fast, frequent, fresh. Four drills carry most of the progress — overhead squat (own the catch), snatch balance (confidence receiving the bar), tall / high-hang snatch (turnover speed), and simply stacking clean reps at a weight you never miss. Add load only when the positions hold on their own.

Or skip it. The snatch isn't mandatory for power — the clean & jerk and power clean deliver nearly all of it. Say the word and I'll drop the snatch entirely and rebuild both days around the clean family.

Making it fit 60 minutes: the two lifting days are the tightest — keep warm-ups to ramp sets on the bar (no wandering), superset the squat with your core or push press to buy time, and if you're still against the clock, the pulls are the first thing to cut. The upper day and every ride slot into an hour with room to spare.

04

Emphasis blocks: without a race, you rotate the dial yourself

No competition means no forced peak — so you create your own progression by biasing the block toward power or toward the engine every 4–6 weeks, then flipping it. Nothing gets neglected; something just leads.

Power-bias block

  • Push the Oly lifts and squats — add load or a set
  • Chase lift PRs while legs are protected
  • Demote Friday's ride to easy Zone 2
  • Physique work holds at maintenance

Engine-bias block

  • Keep the lifts sharp but light (~80%, technical, no grinding)
  • Add a second harder ride or extend the long one
  • Push weekly bike volume and threshold work
  • Watch that W/kg climb

Either block: deload every 4th–5th week — cut volume ~40%, keep the lifts light and fast. The tell that you're overcooking the concurrency: your snatch feels slow and your legs are dead on the bike. When that shows up, pull the bike intensity back first (keep it Zone 2) before you touch the barbell.

05

Bike & physique, handled honestly

Your bike, kept

With no race pulling you toward running, the bike stays exactly what you like it to be — your main cardio, three rides a week. Two easy for base and recovery, one hard on Friday to keep the top end you've built (~3.8 W/kg) from fading.

The only discipline that matters: keep the easy days genuinely easy. That's what lets a hard ride and heavy squats coexist in the same week.

Aesthetics without sabotage

The "touch of vain" is already covered by Thursday plus the optional Saturday pump — deliberately upper-body, where it barely interferes with the lifting or the engine.

The Olympic lifts already build powerful legs, traps and a thick upper back for you. So physique work fills what they miss: arms, chest, lateral delts. Don't add quad hypertrophy — your legs are already buried in squats and pulls.

06

Fuel & recovery

Protein
~175 g/day — roughly 1 g per lb. Anchors muscle and recovery across all this volume.
Calories
For a touch of size: maintenance or a small surplus. Cycling volume leans you out on its own — under-eating loses the muscle you're after.
Creatine
5 g/day, daily. The one supplement with real evidence for power, strength and fullness. Cheap, boring, works.
Sleep
7–9 h. The biggest lever for recovering from concurrent training — and for the CNS the fast lifts depend on.

Assumptions baked in: 6 training days/week at ~60 min each, no competition (general hybrid setup), gym access with a barbell, bumpers and room to drop the lifts, intermediate lifting experience. Change any of those — fewer days, no platform, newer to the classic lifts — and the week gets re-cut around it.

Bike engine (~3.8 W/kg) estimated from your solo Prospect Park ride at 86% max HR on a loaded touring bike. A well-founded estimate, not a lab test — a 20-minute all-out effort would firm it up and set your threshold intervals precisely.
