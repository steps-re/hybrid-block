A week built on the Olympic lifts for power, your bike for the engine, plyos and mobility for the athleticism underneath them, and a touch of physique on top — none of them cannibalizing the others. The organizing rule: barbell speed comes first, so everything else is arranged to keep your legs and nervous system fresh for it.

01

Why it's built this way

The snatch and clean & jerk are speed-and-skill lifts — they need a fresh nervous system and snappy legs, and they're the first thing a fatigued week steals from you. So the whole layout protects the barbell and the explosive work first, then fits the engine and the aesthetics into the space around it.

Skill is the limiter

You can already squat and press more than your clean & jerk needs. So the lifts are run for quality — perfect positions, fast under the bar — and load follows technique, not the other way around.

Explosive work goes first

Plyos and the Olympic lifts live on days 1 & 3, right off rest, when your legs and CNS are freshest. Power and jumps are trained fresh or not at all — you can't grind speed into existence.

One hard ride, well spaced

Your only intense bike session sits far from both lifting days. Hard intervals on tired legs, or heavy squats on ride-fatigued legs, is where the two goals start eating each other.

Mobility is training, not a warm-up

Ankles, hips, thoracic and overhead get real, scheduled time — because those exact positions are what's capping your snatch and jerk. Better positions are more kilos.

02

The training week

Two Olympic days led by a plyo primer and built on squats, one upper strength/physique day, three rides (two easy, one hard), a long weekend ride, and an active recovery day. Each session has a ~60-min core plus a 15–30 min add-on — baked in now, since you're adding volume. Move days to fit your calendar — just keep the two lifting days off the back of your hard ride, and both off the back of rest.

03

The athletic layer: plyos, mobility, bodyweight

This is the part that pays off fastest for you, because it attacks your actual limiter. Speed and positions, not more load. Here's how each piece is dosed so it helps the lifts instead of just adding fatigue.

Plyometrics — quality, never conditioning

Jump primers

Plyos go first, fresh, and in low volume — they wake up your rate of force development, the exact quality the clean and jerk live on. The rule that keeps them useful: every rep is max-intent with full recovery. The moment they turn into a sweaty burn circuit, they stop building power and start eating it.

  • Mon (vertical): pogo hops, then low box jumps — step down, reset, jump again
  • Wed (horizontal): broad jumps, short bounds, med-ball slams/throws
  • Volume: ~15–20 hard contacts total, then move on

Mobility — aimed at your positions

The four that gate the lifts

General stretching is a waste of your time. These four are the ones directly capping your snatch, jerk and front rack — earn them and kilos follow with no new strength.

  • Ankles: deep dorsiflexion → a real bottom position and upright squat
  • Hips: squat-to-stand, 90/90 → depth without the back rounding
  • Thoracic + overhead: the bar stacked over the shoulder, not in front
  • Front rack/wrist: a clean catch that doesn't dump the elbows

Bodyweight & athletic actions

The connective tissue

Built into Friday's add-on and Sunday's play, so it never competes with the barbell. Keeps you springy, well-braced and athletic — not just strong in a straight line.

  • Carries & hangs: farmer carries, dead hangs — grip, traps, shoulder health
  • Core control: hollow holds, L-sits, hanging leg raises
  • Locomotion: bear/lateral crawls, skips, A-marches
  • Single-leg: pistols, step-ups — balance the bilateral squatting
04

The barbell work, and how to run it

The clean & jerk is your power lift, run for clean reps rather than max grinds; the snatch rides along as skill work until it earns the right to be loaded. Pulls build a pull stronger than the lift, and the squats already out-muscle what the clean needs — so they hold and support, they aren't the thing you chase. Percentages are of your best in that lift.

Reading RPE: RPE 8 means "two clean reps left in the tank." For you the hard rule is form caps the set before fatigue does — if a rep degrades, that set is over even if it felt light. You have the strength; you're buying the motor pattern.

Getting better at the snatch: nearly everyone who's "bad at snatches" only ever tries them heavy and occasionally — which is exactly how you don't learn a skill lift. Flip it: light, fast, frequent, fresh. Four drills carry most of the progress — overhead squat (own the catch), snatch balance (confidence receiving the bar), tall / high-hang snatch (turnover speed), and simply stacking clean reps at a weight you never miss. Add load only when the positions hold on their own.

Film it. With form as your limiter, a phone on a tripod is the highest-value tool you have. Record one heavy-ish clean and one snatch each week, from the side. You'll catch the early elbow bend or the bar swinging out long before you feel it — and you can send them to me for a read.

Making it fit 60 minutes: the two lifting days are the tightest — keep the plyo primer short, warm up with ramp sets on the bar (no wandering), superset the squat with your upper hit to buy time, and if you're still against the clock, the pulls are the first thing to cut. The upper day and every ride slot into an hour with room to spare.

05

Adding time: the daily bump and the long day

The volume's in — you've decided to add it, so here's exactly where it goes and the one discipline that keeps it from backfiring. The trap with concurrent training is dumping extra time into more intensity; that's precisely where the two goals start eating each other. Your add-ons buy volume at the easy end and quality at the skill end. Never a second hard thing.

The daily +15–30 min

Rotate it by what the day can absorb without cost:

  • Lifting days: mobility for your positions, or an easy spin
  • Ride days: mobility, or bodyweight core & carries
  • Upper day: easy aerobic volume, or extra arm/delt work

The two highest-value fillers overall: mobility (unlocks kilos) and easy Zone 2 (builds the engine almost for free). Both are near-invisible to recovery.

The weekend long day

Saturday becomes a true long ride — 2 to 4 hours, strictly aerobic. This is the single best thing you can add for the engine, because low-intensity volume barely competes with lifting for recovery. Fatigue on the bike comes from intensity, not duration at an easy pace.

One discipline: it has to stay easy. Fuel it past 90 minutes. The day it drifts into tempo is the day it starts costing you Monday's barbell speed.

The guardrail — watch the data, not your motivation. You're adding this on top of short sleep, so let your own Garmin referee it. Check three numbers weekly from the Jarvis app: morning HRV (a steady drop = fatigue piling up), resting HR (creeping up = under-recovered), and how your bar speed feels Monday. If two of the three slide for a full week, the added volume is the first thing to cut — before the core lifts, before the hard ride. Treat the extra work as a bonus you keep only while you're absorbing it, and the sleep protocol below as non-negotiable now that the load is up.

06

Emphasis blocks & keeping score

No competition means no forced peak — so you create your own progression by biasing the block toward power or toward the engine every 4–6 weeks, then flipping it. Nothing gets neglected; something just leads. And every block ends on a test, so you actually know if it worked.

Power-bias block

  • Push the Oly lifts and squats — add load or a set as form holds
  • Keep the plyo primers sharp; chase lift quality while legs are protected
  • Demote Friday's ride to easy Zone 2, keep Saturday ~90 min
  • Physique work holds at maintenance

Engine-bias block

  • Keep the lifts sharp but light (~80%, technical, no grinding)
  • Add a second harder ride or stretch the Saturday long one
  • Push weekly bike volume and threshold work
  • Watch that W/kg climb

Deload + test, every 4th–5th week. Cut volume ~40% and keep the lifts light and fast. Then, on a fresh day, log the four numbers that tell you the whole story:

1. A crisp clean & jerk single (form intact)  ·  2. Front squat top triple  ·  3. A 20-min bike test → real FTP & W/kg  ·  4. A quick film check on snatch position vs. last time.

The overcooking tell: your snatch feels slow and your legs are dead on the bike. When that shows up, pull the bike intensity back first (keep it Zone 2) before you touch the barbell. At 37 you'll feel the concurrency bite before a 25-year-old would — respect it and the deloads aren't optional.

07

Bike & physique, handled honestly

Your bike, kept

With no race pulling you toward running, the bike stays exactly what you like it to be — your main cardio. Two easy rides for base and recovery, one hard Friday to keep the top end you've built (~3.8 W/kg) from fading, and the new long Saturday to grow the base underneath it.

The only discipline that matters: keep the easy days genuinely easy. That's what lets a hard ride and heavy squats coexist in the same week.

Aesthetics without sabotage

Upper body now gets three touches a week — a superset Monday, one Wednesday, and the full Thursday session — which is what actually drives the "look," since a muscle grows on frequency, not one weekly blast.

The Olympic lifts already build powerful legs, traps and a thick upper back. So physique work fills what they miss: chest, arms, lateral delts, plus the one deliberate hamstring dose. Don't add quad hypertrophy — your legs are already buried in squats and pulls.

09

The nightly sleep protocol

This is now the most important page in the plan. You're adding training load, sleep is your weak link, and no program survives chronic 5-hour nights — the fast lifts run on a rested nervous system and the "look" is built while you sleep. None of this is exotic. It's a handful of small habits, done every night, biggest levers first.

1 · Fix your wake time

Same wake time every day — weekends too. A steady wake time anchors your whole clock and is the single biggest habit for better sleep. Let bedtime drift, but protect the alarm.

2 · Cut caffeine after noon

Caffeine's half-life is ~6 hours, so a 2pm coffee is still a quarter-strong at midnight. This one change alone often adds real deep sleep. Hard stop by lunch.

3 · Empty the head

You run two companies — a racing mind is the likeliest thing keeping you up. Two minutes on paper before bed: tomorrow's top three, plus anything looping. Get it out of your skull and onto the page.

4 · Cold, dark, quiet

Room to ~65°F, blackout or an eye mask, earplugs or white noise. Your core temp has to drop to fall asleep — a hot shower 60–90 min before bed helps by rebound-cooling you after.

Every night, same order

The wind-down ritual (~45 min)

Run the same sequence nightly so your brain learns "this means sleep":

  • ~1 hr out: kill the overhead lights, go dim and warm
  • Screens down or at least off anything stimulating — swap to a book or stretching
  • Brain dump tomorrow's list and any loops
  • Downshift breathing: a few physiological sighs or 4-7-8 to drop the arousal
  • Same bed & wake time — consistency beats duration

Daytime rules that make or break the night

Set up the night early

  • Morning light: get outdoors within ~30–60 min of waking — it sets the clock that runs 16 hours later
  • Alcohol: the worst "nightcap" there is — it wrecks deep & REM sleep and tanks your HRV. You'll see the hit on your watch. Keep it well clear of bedtime
  • Last big meal ~2–3 hr before bed; don't go down stuffed or starving
  • Hard training not within ~3–4 hr of bed where you can help it — it spikes core temp and CNS arousal
Magnesium glycinate
200–400 mg before bed. Mild, safe, well-tolerated — worth a standing try.
Glycine / L-theanine
3 g glycine or 200 mg theanine for a busy head, especially if you had any afternoon caffeine.
Melatonin
Low dose (0.5 mg) only, and for nudging timing — not as a sedative. Skip the 5–10 mg gummies; they're overdosed.
If you wake at 3am
Don't clock-watch. Up more than ~20 min? Get up, dim boring activity, back when sleepy. Keep the wake time fixed regardless.

Pick two to start. Don't try to fix all of this at once — that fails. Start with the fixed wake time and the caffeine cutoff for two weeks, watch your Garmin HRV and resting HR respond, then layer in the wind-down ritual. Small, permanent, stacked.
